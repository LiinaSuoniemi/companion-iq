# Neurotype and Learning-Difference Reference (synthetic)

DISCLAIMER: This is a synthetic, plain-language reference written to show what the tool could ground its support on. It is not complete, not a diagnostic tool, and not medical advice. Profiles are individual; these are patterns, not boxes. Many people have more than one of these at once (co-occurrence is common), and most have a "spiky" profile: strong in some areas, hard in others. None of these is a measure of intelligence or effort. The point is matching the right tool to how a brain works, not labelling a person.

How to use this: when someone describes how their mind works, recognise the pattern, name the strengths first, then offer the support style that tends to fit. Never diagnose. Never imply someone is less capable.

## ADHD (attention regulation difference)

What it is: difficulty regulating attention and executive functions (starting tasks, working memory, sense of time, impulse control), not a lack of attention but trouble directing it on demand.

Common strengths: hyperfocus on interesting work, creativity, fast idea generation, energy, pattern-spotting, crisis performance.

Common challenges: starting boring or vague tasks, finishing, time blindness, forgetting steps, overwhelm from too many options.

What helps: shrink the task to a two-minute start, name only the next single action, externalise everything out of working memory, body doubling, reminders and gentle nudges, reduce the number of decisions.

## Autism spectrum (including what used to be called Asperger's)

What it is: a different way of processing social information, sensory input, and change. (Asperger's is no longer a separate label; it is now considered part of the autism spectrum.)

Common strengths: deep focus on interests, strong pattern and detail recognition, honesty, consistency, expertise built through depth.

Common challenges: reading unspoken social cues, sensory overload (noise, light, texture), ambiguity and sudden change, masking that drains energy.

What helps: clear and literal communication, predictable structure and routine, telling them the plan in advance, reducing sensory and ambiguity load, honouring special interests rather than treating them as a distraction.

## Dyslexia (reading and language-processing difference)

What it is: difficulty with accurate, fluent reading, decoding, and spelling, despite normal or high intelligence.

Common strengths: big-picture thinking, spatial reasoning, storytelling, problem-solving, seeing connections others miss.

Common challenges: slow or effortful reading, spelling, dense walls of text, reading aloud, holding written instructions in mind.

What helps: text-to-speech and audio, plain formatting with white space, short chunks, more time, the say-it-back check for comprehension, never "just read it again faster."

## Dyscalculia (number-processing difference)

What it is: difficulty with number sense, quantity, and arithmetic, the math equivalent of dyslexia.

Common strengths: often strong verbal, creative, or conceptual reasoning even while numbers are hard.

Common challenges: estimating, mental math, sequences, telling time, handling money quickly, remembering number facts.

What helps: visual and concrete representations, step-by-step breakdowns, calculators without shame, removing time pressure.

## Dyspraxia / DCD (motor-coordination difference)

What it is: difficulty with physical coordination and motor planning, which also affects organisation and sequencing.

Common strengths: determination, creative problem-solving, empathy, strong verbal skills.

Common challenges: handwriting, clumsiness, organising belongings and steps, doing things in the right order, tasks with many physical sub-steps.

What helps: break tasks into clear ordered steps, allow extra time, typing instead of handwriting, checklists, reduce simultaneous demands.

## Dysgraphia (writing difference)

What it is: difficulty with the physical act of writing and putting thoughts on the page.

Common strengths: ideas and spoken expression can be rich even when writing is hard.

Common challenges: handwriting, getting started writing, organising thoughts into text, doing the thinking and the writing at the same time.

What helps: typing and speech-to-text, writing scaffolds and templates, separating "get the ideas out" from "make it neat," voice notes first.

## The shared point

Across all of these: the person is not stupid, lazy, or broken. The standard method just was not built for their brain. Give the right tool and the same person who was "failing" starts functioning. Understanding the pattern brings self-empathy, and self-empathy is what lets someone keep striving instead of giving up. The pattern is an explanation, never an excuse.
