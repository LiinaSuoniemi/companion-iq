# Support Techniques Reference (synthetic, for grounding)

This is synthetic reference content for the AI Support demo. It holds general, public self-regulation and focus techniques so the agent can ground its answers and cite a source instead of inventing. No real user data. Not medical advice.

## Grounding when overwhelmed or panicked

5-4-3-2-1 sensory grounding: name five things you can see, four you can touch, three you can hear, two you can smell, one you can taste. Go slowly, one at a time. This pulls attention out of a spiral and into the present room.

Box breathing: breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4. Repeat at least three rounds. Counting and the long exhale settle the nervous system.

Feet on the floor: press both feet down and feel the ground hold you. A simple physical anchor when thoughts race.

Cold water anchor: hold something cold or run cold water over the wrists. The cold sensation interrupts a strong stress response and signals safety.

Time-shrink: when everything feels impossible, shrink the window to the next ten minutes only. "You do not have to solve this now, just the next ten minutes." Triage, not avoidance.

## Starting a task you keep avoiding (focus)

Two-minute start: define the smallest version of the task that takes under two minutes to begin, not to finish. Open the file. Write one sentence. The barrier is starting, not doing.

Body doubling: having another presence nearby, even quietly, reduces the isolation of a hard task and helps initiation. A documented support for ADHD.

Next-action-only: name only the single next physical action, never the whole plan. One step, then the next.

## Organising too many things (planning)

Brain dump: get every task and worry out of your head and onto one surface first, before sorting. Looping in working memory is the load; externalising it relieves the load.

Three buckets: sort items into must happen now (real consequences), should happen soon, and can wait. Most things people feel are urgent are not.

Top three and name what you are not doing: pick at most three from the must bucket. Then explicitly park the rest. Permission to not do things lowers background anxiety.

## Reading and listening comprehension (when your mind wanders)

The wander is a signal, not a failure. When you read a page or listen to a podcast and realise you took nothing in, that is information: your attention drifted. It is normal, especially for minds that work this way.

Roll back, do not push on. Go to the last point you actually remember, then re-read or re-listen from there. Pushing forward past a drift only loses more of the thread.

Use the say-it-back test. Repeat the passage until you can put it in your own words, even one sentence. If you can say what it was about, it landed. If you cannot, read or listen again. That check, not just finishing, is what tells you it stuck.

Accept the extra reps. Some minds need more passes than others to hold the same material. Doing the passes is the method, not a weakness. The work is the same destination by a different route, and that route is valid.

## When to involve a person

Everyday overwhelm needs support and grounding, not escalation. If someone needs a person, the tool can help them reach a pre-set trusted contact with their consent. In genuine danger, the tool stays present, helps the person reach country-correct emergency services on their confirmation, and never contacts anyone on its own. It is a prototype, not a live emergency service.
