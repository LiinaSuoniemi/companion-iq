# Evidence Base (why this works, not just opinion)

This is reference content for the AI Support demo. It lets the agent ground its rationale in real research instead of asserting things, and cite a source. No real user data. Not medical advice.

Note: these are real, well-established research areas and named researchers, described at a general level. They should not be presented as precise statistics, exact dates, or verbatim quotations.

## Claim 1: Different brains need different tools. Struggle is a method mismatch, not laziness or low intelligence.
Support: The neurodiversity paradigm (the term "neurodiversity" was coined by sociologist Judy Singer in the late 1990s) and the social model of disability both reframe difficulty as a mismatch between a person and an environment or method, not a personal deficit. Education research on accommodations shows tailored strategies improve outcomes for the same learner.

## Claim 2: Unsupported neurodivergence is a major under-recognised contributor to adverse life outcomes.
Support: Longitudinal ADHD research, including the work of Russell Barkley, links ADHD with lower educational attainment, employment difficulty, and higher rates of depression and substance use, especially when unsupported. Frame it as a significant contributor, not a single root cause; these outcomes are multifactorial.

## Claim 3: ADHD and learning difficulties are over-represented in prison populations.
Support: Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses find ADHD prevalence among incarcerated people several times higher than the general adult rate. A commonly cited line of work is led by Susan Young and colleagues. (Elevated prevalence is robust; the exact percentage varies by study.)

## Claim 4: Poverty and scarcity narrow thinking to the immediate.
Support: Sendhil Mullainathan and Eldar Shafir, "Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much" (2013). Scarcity imposes a cognitive "bandwidth tax" and produces short-horizon "tunneling," where attention collapses onto the most pressing need.

## Claim 5: Performance under timed, high-pressure conditions does not measure true competence.
Support: Several converging, well-established bodies of research.
- Choking under pressure (Sian Beilock and colleagues): pressure consumes working memory, degrading the very tasks that need it (retrieval, calculation, reasoning). The more someone prepared, the more there is to disrupt.
- Stress and the prefrontal cortex (Amy Arnsten): acute stress impairs the prefrontal cortex, the seat of working memory and retrieval. This is the physiological basis for "the mind goes blank" or "thoughts turn to soup."
- The Yerkes-Dodson law: past a moderate point, rising arousal degrades performance on complex tasks.
- Test anxiety research (e.g. Moshe Zeidner): high test anxiety impairs exam performance independently of how much the person knows.
- Foreign language anxiety (Horwitz, Horwitz and Cope, 1986): anxiety disrupts second-language production even in competent speakers. This explains "forgetting" how to speak a fluent non-native language, and over-monitoring grammar, in front of native speakers.

Implication: a timed exam or high-pressure interview measures performance under those conditions, not underlying ability. The person who blanks may be equally or more capable than the one who performs smoothly under pressure. Real software work is mostly asynchronous, collaborative, and spread over days, conditions where many of these people excel.

## Research anchors by neurotype (for equality, not only ADHD)
Real research areas so the tool can speak to more than one neurotype, at a general level.
- ADHD: executive function and working-memory differences (Russell Barkley); emotional regulation under stress.
- Dyslexia: phonological-processing difficulty alongside intact intelligence (Sally Shaywitz and the phonological-deficit research).
- Autism: differences in social communication and sensory processing; the high hidden cost of masking or camouflaging (research by Hull, Mandy and colleagues); the double empathy problem (Damian Milton), which reframes social difficulty as a two-way mismatch rather than a one-sided deficit.
- Dyscalculia: difficulty with number sense and the approximate number system (Brian Butterworth).
- Dyspraxia / DCD: developmental coordination disorder, a recognised motor-planning condition.
- Dysgraphia: difficulty with the transcription and motor side of writing.

## Vision, not evidence
The chain "relieve scarcity, then people have capacity to act collectively, and that helps the planet" is a hopeful hypothesis, not a proven causal claim. Present it as long-term vision, never as established fact.
